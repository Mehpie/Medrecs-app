Cache-Augmented Multi-Document Causal Extraction System and method with Relational Vector Indexing, Controlled Vocabulary Enrichment, and Citation Provenance 
Background
Technical Field
    [0001] The present disclosure relates generally to computer-implemented systems and methods for processing and analyzing data originating from bulk documents, and more particularly, relates to computer-implemented systems and methods for processing and analyzing data originating from bulk documents in cache-augmented generation architecture using large language models (LLM).  
Description of the Related Art
    [0002] Existing systems used for medical document analysis include LLM pipelines, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems, and human expert review. However, these current systems suffer from several technical limitations when applied to multi-document analysis, such as medical-legal analysis. 
    [0003] Legacy document summarization systems that are configured to produce descriptive summaries of medical records do not generate causal narratives linking findings across temporally separated documents, do not cross-reference generated claims against published medical literature, and do not produce page-level citations grounded in vector-space distance. Such legacy systems produce catalog-style summaries of individual records rather than cross-document causal analysis.
    [0004] For one, these known systems rely on fragment-level retrieval, which loses cross-document causal context and cannot scale with increasing complexity of raw data. RAG systems retrieve chunks based on semantic similarity to a query but do not maintain relational awareness between entities or objects that appear in different documents or on distant pages. Traditional review methods process information sequentially or linearly, that is, document by document and record by record, one item at a time. Typically, these linear review layers produce summaries, AI-generated or otherwise. These traditional linear methods are capable of organizing data by date or source but miss cross-record relationships. For example, a causal relationship between a COVID-19 history recorded in a billing note and a brain fog symptom documented in an examination note three years later cannot be identified by these current systems, which retrieve each document independently. 
    [0005] In the use case of medical related litigation, thousands of pages of medical records describe events but fail to clarify how those events connect to injury, causation, or outcome. A system is needed to reveal the critical relationships hidden inside unstructured documentation that standard linear review fails to capture and that may be exploitable by opposing counsel.  Moreover, various disciplines rely on document analysis to prepare detailed, citation-supported reports for use in contexts such as investigations, litigation, and expert testimony. However, existing systems, including general-purpose large language models, are known to generate inaccurate or fabricated citations, known as hallucinations, resulting in unreliable reports. Without a mechanism that preserves page-level provenance through the vectorization and generation process, language models produce outputs that cannot be automatically verified by the system against source documents. Such outputs may be inadmissible in legal proceedings. Prior citation mechanisms in LLM systems rely on keyword matching, retrieved chunk provenance, or direct quotation. A 
    [0006] Furthermore, standard embedding systems discard structural metadata altogether. Off-the-shelf text embedding pipelines convert document text to vectors but do not encode structural metadata (page number, document type, or other necessary and useful contextual information, such as clinical or legal entity class) as queryable relational properties. This makes it impossible to retrieve or identify which page of which document contains a finding causally linked to a particular symptom. 
    [0007] Lastly, another known drawback of legacy systems is that single-document analysis misses longitudinal causation, almost by definition. Medical-legal cases require analysis across years of records from multiple providers, yet current systems are limited by their single-document structure and fail to process, let alone recognize, trends or causation over time. A system is needed that is able to address these and other needs.
Brief Summary 
    [0008] Briefly stated, the present disclosure provides a cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system and method that (1) receives and ingests a batch of cache-augmented multiple documents, (2) causally extracts and organizes data from the cache-augmented multiple documents while preserving section-specific metadata, (3) maps relationships with the organized data using relational vector indexing (4) encodes relational properties into causal-candidate pairs, (5) determines page-level citation-supported provenance for the causal-candidate pairs, and (6) produces a citation-supported output reflecting cross-document causal relationships found within the data. 
    [0009] In some embodiments, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system employs a custom extraction and vectorization pipeline that encodes extracted text units along with their respective structural document metadata and domain-specific entity tags. In another aspect of some embodiments, the system constructs a relational cache using a Cache-Augmented Generation (CAG) architecture that operates on a full relational vector representation of all of the multiple documents simultaneously rather than retrieving fragments. In still another aspect of some embodiments, the system employs an external large language model that uses engineered prompts to perform causal reasoning across the relational cache.  In yet another aspect of some embodiments, the system applies a controlled vocabulary of domain-specific forced tags to enrich the generated output and identify causal-candidate entity pairs. Furthermore, in another aspect of some embodiments, the system assigns page-level citations to every claim in the report by computing vector-space distances between claim embeddings and page-level entries in the relational cache.

    [0052] Methodology
    [0053] The system 100 carries out a method of creating relational intelligence from fragmented data, transforming unorganized information into a network model capable of producing actionable insight across any domain. The method moves through six operations as described in detail below.
    [0054] Batch Document Ingestion and Extraction 510
    [0055] In one embodiment shown in FIG. 5, documents concerning the same case, event, or instance are uploaded simultaneously as a batch of documents 102 to the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 by a user via a user terminal. The system 100 accepts heterogeneous document types including: Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan (SOAP) notes, Electroencephalography (EEG) reports, Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) notes, billing records, examination notes, unstructured PDF records, and other similar standard types of documents and reports. In some embodiments, a custom extraction pipeline  of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 is configured to convert each document from its original or source format into structured text, preserving at least one of the following metadata for each extracted text unit: source document identifier; page number within the source document; document type classification (for example, clinical, legal, billing, or imaging); section structure (where identifiable from document formatting); and other similar applicable standard types of metadata. 
    [0056] This metadata preservation at extraction time provides a technological improvement for subsequent operations such as data manipulation and generating reports 108 with accurate citations to metadata elements including, e.g., correct page numbers. The extracted text units having preserved metadata is a technological improvement over current insufficient systems where standard PDF extraction pipelines discard page metadata during text extraction. The extraction pipeline of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 explicitly retains and encodes the respective document metadata as a structured attribute of each extracted text unit.
    [0057] Custom Vectorization with Entity Encoding 520
    [0058] Next in the embodiment shown in FIG. 5, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 vectorizes the extracted metadata-text data or metadata-text units from the batch of documents 102 to generate embeddings or vector representations of the data that encode both the semantic content of the text and its associated structural metadata. The resulting vector representation for each text unit includes at least one of the following: a semantic embedding of the text content; domain-specific or disciplinary entity tags, (e.g., (1) clinical entity tags include symptoms, diagnoses, medications, diagnostic findings, anatomical references, temporal markers, and other similar standard clinical categories, or (2) legal entity tags include injury events, causation claims, functional limitations, daily life impact descriptors, and other similar industry-standard useful or helpful legal categories); and structural metadata, e.g., page number, document type, source document ID, and section identifier.  
    [0059] The combination of semantic embedding with industry-specific or domain-specific entity tagging at the vectorization stage is key for the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 to subsequently identify significant relationships amongst the data. This combination of semantic content and named entities is an improvement over the current known systems because standard medical Natural Language Processing (NLP) pipelines encode either semantic content or named entities, but not both simultaneously in a form that supports relational querying. By encoding both in the same vector representation, the system 100 enables queries of the form, for example: “find all clinical findings of type [X] that are structurally associated with legal entities of type [Y] across all documents in this case.”
    [0060] Relational Vector Cache Construction 530
    [0061] In another aspect of the embodiment shown in FIG. 5, vectorized or embedded units are stored with relational properties in a suitable vector database or embedding database configured to store vector representations. In some embodiments, the database is a Qdrant-based vector database. The relational cache is constructed to support multi-variable relational querying across the full batch of documents 102. No document in the batch of documents 102 is left unrelated in order to provide a comprehensive analysis of the entire evidence records. 
    [0062] In some embodiments, the database schema encodes relationships between vector entries along specified dimensions, such as temporal, causal candidate, cross-document, cross-modal, page-provenance, and other similar dimensions useful to the discipline and user. A temporal relationship is an ordering of findings by date of documentation across all source documents. A causal candidate is an individual entity tag belonging to an entity class that the controlled vocabulary recognizes as a potential participant in a causal relationship. A causal-candidate pair is the confirmed combination of two causal candidates whose entity classes have an established causal relationship as defined by the controlled vocabulary, that is, where one entity class is a known causal precursor of the other. The controlled vocabulary is the authority that determines which entity class combinations constitute valid causal-candidate pairs.  Without a controlled vocabulary entry confirming the relationship, two causal candidates remain unrelated.  A cross-document relationship links findings in different source documents belonging to the same patient record. A cross-modal relationship links findings from different diagnostic modalities (e.g., EEG finding → clinical symptom).  A page-provenance or page-attribution relationship involves bidirectional mapping between each vector entry and its source page number and document identifier.
    [0063] These relationships or relational structures constitute the “multi-variable mindmap” that is necessary for the generative model to operate in this unique relational way. The construction of this relational cache is what distinguishes the approach of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 from both standard RAG (which retrieves independently) and standard CAG (which caches without relational structure). The relational properties of the relational cache enable the system 100 to identify causal relationships across the batch of documents 102 rather than only semantic similarities. 
    [0064] Insight or Inference on Cached Relational Structure 540
    [0065] Referring again to the embodiment shown in FIG. 5, a large language model 104 that is suitable to the specific use-case or domain is used as the generative component or module of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100. In at least one embodiment, Google’s MedGemma language model 104, which is optimized for medical comprehension, is used in the medical domain. The large language model 104 operates on the full cached relational structure of the relational cache rather than just on retrieved fragments. In this Cache-Augmented Generation (CAG) architecture, the large language model 104 has access to the complete relational representation of all documents 102 in the case during the inference process, rather than receiving only the top-k semantically similar chunks as in standard RAG. 
    [0066] In one or more embodiments, the large language model 104 is directed by a set of engineered prompts that are hard-coded in the codebase of the system 100. These engineered prompts instruct the large language model 104 to perform specific reasoning tasks over the relational cache built from the batch of documents 102. These tasks include: (1) longitudinal analysis by identifying how findings evolve across time within the full document set; (2) causal relationship identification by identifying which earlier findings are causally implicated in later documented symptoms, drawing on both the relational structure of the relational cache and the medical knowledge of the model 104; (3) objective metric extraction that surfaces specific quantitative findings (scores, measurements, test results, etc.) with their source citations; and (4) direct quotation to produce verbatim quotes from source documents with page-level provenance.
    [0067] The causal output of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 is not produced by prompting alone. If a large language model 104 was presented with the same prompts without the relational vector cache, it would produce generic causal reasoning based on its training data, not patient-specific causal chains grounded in the specific documents 102 of the case. 
    [0068] The causal relationships identified by the large language model 104 emerge from the intersection of three components operating together: (1) the relational metadata encoded in the relational cache (which identifies causal-candidate entity pairs); (2) the engineered prompts (which direct the model to reason causally over the cache structure); and (3) the large language model’s domain knowledge (which validates whether a causal candidate pair is plausible). No single component produces the causal output. The novel output emerges from the combination of these three components. 
    [0069] Additionally, some embodiments of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 include fine-tuning the large language model 104 on a specific dataset of medical-legal document that pairs with verified causal annotations. This fine-tuning further improves causal identification accuracy beyond the current base-model-plus-prompting approach.
    [0070] Controlled Vocabulary Forced-Tag Enrichment 550 
    [0071] In another aspect of the embodiment shown in FIG. 5, some domains include a technological improvement from the use of a domain-specific vocabulary. After the large language model 104 generates its initial first output over the relational cache, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 applies a domain-specific or controlled vocabulary enrichment layer. This vocabulary enrichment layer includes a predefined taxonomy of domain-specific concepts or “forced tags” that are systematically applied to the generated output to identify gaps and opportunities for enrichment. The controlled vocabulary taxonomy defines which entity combinations are valid causal-candidate pairs for forced-tag application. This taxonomy is unique to the relevant field of the use case and is a custom-made classification system built for the specific domain.  
    [0072] In some embodiments, the forced-tag enrichment operates as follows. The cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 scans the first output to identify entity tags present in the generated causal claims. For each identified entity tag, the system 100 queries the controlled vocabulary to determine whether a valid causal-candidate pair exists for that entity. Where the controlled vocabulary identifies a valid causal-candidate pair that is absent from or underrepresented in the first output, the system 100 flags that pair as a “gap” and generates a supplemental causal claim or enrichment entry directed to that pair. The system 100 then queries external database 106 for supporting information corresponding to each flagged causal-candidate pair. Claims supported by retrieved literature are retained and enriched with citations.  In contrast, claims for which no supporting literature is retrieved are flagged in the enriched output so that the user may exercise independent judgment as to their inclusion in the final report 108. The result of controlled vocabulary forced-tag enrichment is the enriched output, which comprises the causal claims of the first output as supplemented and validated by the controlled vocabulary and supporting literature retrieved from the external database 106, for inclusion in the report 108.
    [0073] The controlled vocabulary is domain-specific and is not publicly available or derived from a public ontology. In the medical-legal domain, the controlled vocabulary taxonomy is necessary in order to draw useful, significant, and valid connections between clinical and legal domains. This legal-medical taxonomy specific to traumatic brain injury (TBI) and personal injury (PI) cases was developed by a board-certified functional neurologist (FACFN, FABBIR) and encodes the domain expertise of a practicing specialist in functional neurology and brain injury rehabilitation. The taxonomy defines which clinical entity combinations are medically valid causal precursor/sequela pairs for legal documentation purposes.  In other embodiments, the taxonomy covers additional medical specialties beyond functional neurology and TBI. The taxonomy is the bridge between the relational structure of the relational cache and sources outside of the original document upload 102, such as the external database 106.
    [0074] Vector-Space Distance Mapping for Page-Level Citation 560
    [0075] Referring again to the embodiment shown in FIG. 5, the next stage of the pipeline produces the cited, legally defensible output. For each claim or finding in the enriched output, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 computes the vector-space distance between the claim embedding and all page-level vector entries in the relational cache.  Notably, the relational cache was built from the batch of documents 102. The page entry with the minimum distance (highest semantic similarity) in vector space is assigned as the citation for that claim, along with its source document identifier and page number. 
    [0076] This mechanism produces citations that are grounded in semantic similarity to the source material rather than in keyword matching or positional proximity. For example, a claim about a causal relationship between COVID-19 history and subsequent cognitive symptoms is cited to the page in the source documents 102 where the most semantically relevant documentation of that relationship exists regardless of whether the same keywords appear on that page.
    [0077] Generate Citation Enriched Report 570
    [0078] In another aspect of some embodiments shown in FIG. 5, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 compiles the enriched output and page-level citations into the report 108 and delivers the report 108 to the user.  In one such embodiment, the citation system produces the report 108 in the following form: each generated claim is accompanied by a source reference in the format [Document Type, Document ID, Page N].  This enables a user, such as an attorney or court, to locate the source of every assertion in the report 108 by turning directly to the cited page.
    [0079] FIG. 6 illustrates another method executed by the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 to produce the report 108 from the batch of documents 102.  At operation 610, the system 100 receives, via a computing device, a batch of documents uploaded by a user.  At operation 620, the system 100 converts, using an extraction pipeline, each document of the batch into structured text units preserving, for each structured text unit, associated metadata comprising at least one a source document identifier and a page number. At operation 630, the system 100 embeds the structured text units in a manner that encodes semantic text content, metadata, and domain-specific entity tags.  At operation 640, the system 100 constructs a relational cache by storing the embedded structured text units in a vector database with relational properties encoding temporal, causal-candidate, cross-document, and page-provenance relationships among the vector representations.
    [0080] At operation 650, the system 100 performs Cache-Augmented Generation (CAG) inference by prompting a large language model using engineered prompts to analyze the relational cache and generate a first output comprising causal claims derived from relationships among vector representations across the batch of documents.  At operation 660, the system 100 compares the first output to a controlled vocabulary of predefined valid causal entity pairs of more than one causal-candidate, retrieving supporting information from one or more third-party databases for each identified pair, and generating a second output.  At operation 670, the system 100 computes, for each claim in the second output, a vector-space distance between a claim embedding and page-level vector representations in the relational cache, and assigning a page-level citation to each claim based on a minimum computed distance.  Finally, at operation 680, the system 100 generates a report comprising the second output and the page-level citations.

    [0086] Demonstrated Example Use in Medical Domain of Longitudinal Causal Analysis
    [0087] The following describes a representative test case demonstrating the ability of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 to identify causal relationships across temporally and documentarily separated clinical findings. Specific patient identifiers and case details have been anonymized. 
    [0088] The input material included in a merged medical record batch of documents 102 include one or more of the following document types: (1) SOAP notes spanning multiple clinical visits over a multi-year period; (2) billing records from primary care and specialist encounters; (3) EEG reports from neurological evaluation; (4) MRI imaging notes; and (6) examination notes from neurological and functional assessments. In some embodiments, the total document length is within the 700-1,200 page range characteristic of TBI/PI cases processed by the system 100.
    [0089] The cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 identified a causal chain linking a documented COVID-19 infection history (appearing in early clinical records) to a subsequently documented brain fog and cognitive impairment symptom cluster (appearing in neurological evaluation records approximately three years later).
    [0090] This causal identification required: (1) recognition of the COVID-19 history entry as a clinical entity tag of type [infectious disease / systemic inflammatory event] in the early billing and SOAP records; (2) recognition of the brain fog / cognitive impairment documentation as a clinical entity tag of type [neurological symptom cluster / cognitive deficit] in the later neurological evaluation records; (3) identification of this entity pair as a causal candidate pair by the controlled vocabulary forced-tag system; (4) retrieval of abstracts from the external database 106 supporting the causal relationship between post-COVID-19 infection and neurological cognitive sequelae; and (5) generation of a causal claim linking the two findings, enriched with citations and attributed to the specific source pages where each finding was documented. 
    [0091] The cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 produced a structured report 108 containing: (1) a causal narrative linking the COVID-19 infection history to the subsequent cognitive symptoms, with the causal mechanism explained in terms consistent with the supporting literature from the external database 106; (2) page-level citations for the COVID-19 history documentation (source document, page number); (3) page-level citations for the brain fog symptom documentation (source document, page number); (4) abstract citations supporting the biological plausibility of the causal relationship; and (5) structured answers to a pre-defined set of report questions covering longitudinal history, causal relationships, objective metrics, and daily life impact. 
    [0092] A standard RAG system presented with the same documents would fail to identify this causal relationship because the COVID-19 history appears in billing records and SOAP notes from Year 1, while the brain fog symptom cluster appears in neurological evaluation notes from Year 4. A RAG system queried about neurological symptoms would retrieve the Year 4 neurological evaluation notes as the most semantically similar documents. It would not retrieve the Year 1 billing records, because a billing record entry for a COVID-19 infection is not semantically similar to a query about neurological symptoms.  A human investigator would almost certainly fail to identify this causal relationship for similar reasons.
    [0093] Notably, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 identifies this relationship because: (a) the COVID-19 infection is encoded as a causal-candidate precursor entity tag in the relational cache at ingestion time; (b) the controlled vocabulary forced-tag system identifies the COVID-19 → cognitive impairment pair as a valid causal candidate; and (c) the large language model 104, operating on the full relational cache, can traverse the causal candidate relationship across the temporal and documentary gap between the two findings. 
    [0094] This output cannot be produced by prompting a generic LLM with the raw documents 102, because the causal candidate pair identification depends on the relational structure of the relational cache, not on the semantic content of any individual document.  As mentioned above, a human investigator would almost certainly fail to identify this causal relationship for similar reasons.
    [0095] This demonstrated example is merely one way of using the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 in the medical domain. This medical document intelligence system 100 ingests various medical evidence or heterogeneous clinical records as a batch of documents 102 including SOAP notes, EEG reports, MRI notes, billing records, examination notes, including symptoms from patient reports, diagnostic tests and imaging, observations from physician notes, treatments and interventions, and outcomes measured by results and progression. 
    [0096] The cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 is configured to carry out cross-temporal analysis by evaluating how much medical information connects across time and across providers, mapping the evolution of clinical findings through the full record to surface patterns that span years, specialties, and documentation styles. The system 100 is also configured to (1) carry out injury progression mapping that reveals how injuries develop and are documented over time, (2) identify critical inflection points in the clinical narrative, (3) identify gaps in the clinical narrative, and (4) identify moments where the record diverges from a claimed timeline. Using this relational information, the system 100 is configured to detect contradictions and inconsistencies within the medical evidence and records. For example, conflicting diagnoses, divergent treatment paths, and documentation gaps that may affect a case are all examples of anomalies. Detection of these contradictions earlier rather than later is key to correcting incorrect or incomplete diagnoses and/or enabling enhanced preparation for cross-examination in a legal proceeding. 
    [0097] Example Cross-Disciplinary Use in Medical-Legal Matters
    [0098] In some embodiments, an additional layer is added to the prior example.  In such an embodiment, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 operates at the intersection of legal and clinical evidence by transforming medical records into structured evidence intelligence, rather than chronological summaries to produce legally defensible causation narratives with page-level cited provenance.
    [0099] The system 100 employs a combination of: (1) a custom extraction and vectorization pipeline that encodes both clinical and legal entities with structural document metadata; (2) a Cache-Augmented Generation (CAG) architecture that operates on a full relational vector representation of all documents simultaneously rather than retrieving fragments; (3) MedGemma as the generative language model, directed by engineered prompts to perform causal reasoning across the cached relational structure; (4) a controlled vocabulary forced-tag enrichment layer cross-referenced against PubMed abstracts; and (5) a vector-space distance mapping system that assigns page-level citations to every generated claim. The result is a report that is structurally distinct from both human-authored summaries and generic LLM outputs because it is cited, causal, cross-referenced against published medical literature, and defensible in a legal proceeding.
    [0100] The cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 is capable of identifying causal relationships across disciplines because, in addition to the embeddings encoding respective metadata and semantics, the embeddings also encode the structural elements having assigned legal entity tags.  Additionally, the structural elements having assigned medical entity tags. Accessible via the CAG architecture, the system 100 is able to identify links, connections, pairs, and causal relationships amongst the entity tags and structural elements. These embeddings, in combination with the engineered prompts and MedGemma’s medical domain knowledge, render the system 100 capable of identifying valid, medically plausible, and supported causal candidate pairs among the documents. 
    [0101] The controlled vocabulary taxonomy specific to this cross-disciplinary domain of legal and medical is necessary in order to draw useful, significant, and valid connections between the different domains. The taxonomy enables the system 100 to link entity combinations as valid causal-candidate pairs for forced-tag application. This legal-medical taxonomy specific to TBI and PI cases was developed by a board-certified functional neurologist (FACFN, FABBIR) and encodes the domain expertise of a practicing specialist in functional neurology and brain injury rehabilitation. The taxonomy defines which clinical entity combinations are medically valid causal precursor/sequela pairs for legal documentation purposes. 
    [0102] In other embodiments, the taxonomy covers additional medical specialties beyond functional neurology and TBI.  The taxonomy is the bridge between the relational structure of the vector cache and sources outside of the original document upload. In this embodiment, the source is PubMed which the system 100 uses to create a PubMed enrichment layer. The taxonomy is not a publicly available or derived from ontology and is provided by the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100.  The system 100 applies the controlled vocabulary enrichment layer to the initial output over the relational cache. This vocabulary enrichment layer includes a predefined taxonomy of medical-legal concepts, or “forced tags,” that are systematically applied to the generated output to identify gaps and opportunities for enrichment. 
    [0103] For each causal claim in the generated output, the system 100: (1) extracts the clinical entities involved in the causal relationship (e.g. “COVID-19 infection” and “brain fog / cognitive impairment”); (2) queries the PubMed database by retrieving abstracts matching the identified clinical entity pair and causal relationship type; (3) cross-references the retrieved PubMed abstracts against the causal claim in the generated output; (4) enriches the output with supporting literature citations where PubMed abstracts validate the causal claim; and (5) flags causal claims for which no supporting PubMed literature exists, reducing the risk of unsupported assertions in the final report.
    [0104] The combination of semantic vector embeddings with simultaneously encoded clinical entity tags and legal entity tags on structural document elements (stored in a Qdrant relational vector database) enables the system 100 to draw useful connections (relationships) to produce a citation-supported report. This enables the system 100 to encode both clinical and legal entity classes as first-class relational properties of a vector cache for multi-document medical-legal analysis. This dual encoding is what enables the system 100 to answer queries that cross domains including clinical and legal conceptual boundaries, e.g., identifying which clinical findings (clinical entity) support which elements of a negligence claim (legal entity).
    [0105] Some embodiments of the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 include fine-tuning MedGemma on a specific dataset of medical-legal document pairs with verified causal annotations, to improve causal identification accuracy beyond the current base-model-plus-prompting approach. 
    [0106] The added layer of computing vector-space distance between generated claims and page-level cache entries to assign legally defensible page citations enables the system 100 to generate sophisticated and accurate medical-legal reports. The citation mechanism of the system 100 assigns citations based on semantic similarity in vector space, producing citations that are grounded in meaning, rather than merely surface form. This mechanism enables the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 to generate reports where every claim is traceable to a specific page in a specific source document, meeting the evidentiary standard required for legal proceedings. 
    [0107] In some embodiments, the cache-augmented multi-document causal extraction system 100 applies CAG architecture specifically to multi-document medical-legal analysis, where the cache contains the full relational vector representation of 700–1,200 pages of heterogeneous medical records. The application of CAG to these domains, with the specific relational structure described, produces qualitatively different outputs from RAG-based approaches, i.e., causal chains that span multiple documents and years of patient history, rather than answers grounded in the most similar retrieved fragment.

Abstract of the Disclosure
A computer-implemented system and method configured to generate a citation-rich report detailing findings of significant relationships amongst bulk documents. The system carries out a method of extracting and processing data from the bulk documents, by generating vector representations including section-specific metadata elements and domain-specific entity tags, into a relational cache. The system may include a controlled vocabulary that defines some entity tags and helps identifying the significant relationships. The system is configured to interact with third party websites and large language models to cross-reference the findings and obtain scholarly support for the same. The system includes engineered prompts for interacting with the LLM to help identify significant relationships found in the relational cache, as well as a vector-space distance mapping operation for identifying stronger findings based on close relationship values.
